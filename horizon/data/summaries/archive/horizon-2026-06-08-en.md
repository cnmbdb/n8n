## [Precise Gene Editing in Human Embryos Raises Designer Baby Fears](https://www.ithome.com/0/961/382.htm) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers led by Dieter Egli of Columbia University and Nathan Treff of Nucleus Genomics used base editing to precisely modify genes in human embryos for the first time, targeting mutations linked to heart disease and blood disorders. This breakthrough could pave the way for preventing inherited genetic diseases in embryos, but it reignites ethical debates over 'designer babies' and the slippery slope toward eugenics. Base editing is more precise than CRISPR because it does not cut DNA, but the study found mosaicism in nearly 80% of edited embryos, meaning not all cells were corrected. The research was funded in part by Genomic Prediction and Nucleus Genomics, companies involved in embryo screening and polygenic risk assessment.

Base editing is a next-generation technique that changes a single DNA base without breaking the double helix, reducing off-target effects compared to CRISPR. Earlier studies had used base editing in embryos, but this is the first reported case of precise editing for multiple disease-related genes simultaneously.
